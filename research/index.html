Research

Research areas and experimental approaches

Ryu Lab investigates how commensal microbes in the gut and skin tune immune homeostasis, tissue-resident cell programs, and inflammatory disease susceptibility.

Themes

Three core questions

Each block explains what the lab studies and why it matters.

Gut microbiome and immune homeostasis

We examine how commensal microbes help establish balanced host immune responses and maintain healthy tissue physiology.

gut microbiome immune homeostasis host physiology

Skin microbiota and inflammatory disease

We study how skin dysbiosis and early-life microbial priming contribute to allergic inflammation and disease susceptibility.

skin dysbiosis allergic inflammation early life

Tissue-resident cell regulation by microbiota

We aim to define resident cell populations whose development, differentiation, and function are controlled by commensal microbes.

resident cells development microbiota

Framework

From microbial cues to disease mechanisms

Across projects, the lab connects microbial signals, tissue programs, and measurable immune outcomes.

Step 1

Commensal communities

We define relevant gut and skin microbes, their metabolites, and the timing of host exposure.

Step 2

Tissue programs

We examine epithelial signals, tissue-resident immune cells, and developmental programs shaped by commensals.

Step 3

Experimental systems

Germ-free and gnotobiotic models, inflammatory disease models, and microbiome-informed analyses provide mechanistic tests.

Step 4

Disease relevance

We ask how microbial regulation influences homeostasis, allergic inflammation, infection responses, and tissue function.

Methods

Experimental platforms

The lab combines controlled microbial model systems with tissue-level immune phenotyping and microbiome-informed analysis.

Model systems

  • Germ-free and gnotobiotic animal models
  • Defined commensal colonization systems
  • Dysbiosis-driven inflammatory disease models

Analysis and readouts

  • Microbiome-based functional analysis
  • Host immune and tissue phenotyping
  • Comparative studies across gut and skin systems

Why it matters

From commensal balance to disease mechanisms

Our work aims to clarify how tissue-resident cells and host-microbiome interactions influence homeostasis, inflammatory disease, and long-term tissue function.
