Research

Research areas and experimental approaches

Ryu Lab investigates how commensal microbes in the gut and skin tune immune homeostasis, tissue-resident cell programs, and inflammatory disease susceptibility.

Vision

Principal research interests

This section outlines the scientific vision that connects the lab's microbiome research platforms, mechanistic questions, and long-term goals.

The mammalian gut harbors a remarkably diverse community of commensal microbes that far outnumber host cells. A major goal of the lab is to understand how this co-evolved microbial ecosystem supports host immune homeostasis and contributes to the development and maintenance of tissue programs, including the enteric nervous system.

As part of this long-term research direction, Professor Ji-Hwan Ryu first developed Drosophila gnotobiotic animal model systems and used them to reveal foundational mechanisms in host-microbiome interactions, including the work reported in Science (2008). As an independent principal investigator, he further expanded the field by developing experimental platforms such as germ-free and gnotobiotic mouse systems colonized with defined commensal microbes, as well as models of gut and skin dysbiosis-driven inflammatory disease, including work highlighted in Cell Host & Microbe (2024).

Looking ahead, the lab aims to identify and functionally define tissue-resident cell populations whose development and differentiation are regulated by gut and skin commensal microbiota. Defining how these cells influence host homeostasis and disease remains a major focus of the group's future research.

Framework

From microbial cues to disease mechanisms

Across projects, the lab connects microbial signals, tissue programs, and measurable immune outcomes.

Step 1

Commensal communities

We define relevant gut and skin microbes, their metabolites, and the timing of host exposure.

Step 2

Tissue programs

We examine epithelial signals, tissue-resident immune cells, and developmental programs shaped by commensals.

Step 3

Experimental systems

Germ-free and gnotobiotic models, inflammatory disease models, and microbiome-informed analyses provide mechanistic tests.

Step 4

Disease relevance

We ask how microbial regulation influences homeostasis, allergic inflammation, infection responses, and tissue function.

Environment

A collaborative and well-equipped research setting

The institutional environment helps the lab connect mechanistic basic science with clinically relevant questions and robust experimental execution.

Clinical and basic science collaboration

Because the institute is closely connected to Severance Hospital, the lab can readily collaborate with clinical faculty as well as basic science professors across diverse specialties. This makes it easier to develop interdisciplinary projects that connect mechanistic microbiome research with translational and disease-oriented questions.

Strength in animal models

The institute maintains a germ-free mouse facility and provides flexible access to gene-modified transgenic mouse lines as well as SPF mouse resources. These strengths make the environment especially well suited for animal experiments that require controlled microbial exposure and disease modeling.

Immune cell analysis platforms

The lab also benefits from access to up-to-date FACS instrumentation that supports detailed immune cell analysis and sorting. Together, these platforms give researchers the technical flexibility to pursue independent and high-resolution studies of host-microbe and immune interactions.

Why it matters

From commensal balance to disease mechanisms

Our work aims to clarify how tissue-resident cells and host-microbiome interactions influence homeostasis, inflammatory disease, and long-term tissue function.
